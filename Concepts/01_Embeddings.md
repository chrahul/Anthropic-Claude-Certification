## Key Architectural Details (CCA-F Perspective)

Embeddings are typically generated by specialized embedding models rather than by generative large language models themselves. In Claude-based architectures, Claude's primary responsibility is reasoning, generation, tool orchestration, and agent execution. Knowledge retrieval is usually handled by a separate embedding model and vector search layer. Anthropic commonly references external embedding providers such as Voyage AI for retrieval pipelines, although the architectural principle is more important than any specific vendor: embedding generation and language generation are distinct capabilities that are often implemented using different models.

An embedding is represented as a dense numerical vector containing hundreds or thousands of dimensions. Modern embedding models commonly produce vectors ranging from approximately 1,000 to 1,500 dimensions, although the exact dimensionality depends on the model. These dimensions should not be interpreted individually. Instead, the complete vector collectively represents the semantic meaning of the text. Similar concepts tend to occupy nearby regions of vector space, enabling efficient semantic retrieval.

Once embeddings have been generated, similarity measurement becomes the foundation of retrieval. The most widely used technique is cosine similarity, which measures the angle between two vectors rather than their absolute magnitude. This makes it particularly effective for semantic search because the focus is on similarity of meaning rather than document length. A higher cosine similarity score indicates stronger semantic relatedness between two pieces of text. While alternative metrics such as Euclidean distance exist, cosine similarity remains the dominant approach in production retrieval systems because it provides more stable semantic comparisons across documents of varying sizes.

A critical distinction exists between word embeddings and sentence or document embeddings. Early embedding approaches such as Word2Vec generated a separate vector for each word. These representations were context-independent, meaning a word such as "bank" would receive the same embedding regardless of whether it referred to a financial institution or the side of a river. Modern retrieval systems instead generate embeddings for entire sentences, paragraphs, chunks, or documents. Because the surrounding context is included, the resulting embeddings better capture the intended meaning and are significantly more useful for enterprise retrieval workloads.

In production Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems, documents are rarely embedded as a single large unit. Instead, they are first divided into smaller chunks before embedding generation. Chunking is one of the most important architectural decisions in a retrieval pipeline because it directly affects retrieval quality. Smaller chunks generally improve retrieval precision by narrowing the scope of retrieved content, while larger chunks preserve more context but may introduce irrelevant information. The optimal chunking strategy depends on the domain, document structure, and retrieval objectives. For this reason, chunking is considered a foundational prerequisite to effective embedding and retrieval design.

From a CCA-F perspective, embeddings should not be viewed as a standalone AI capability. They are the enabling layer that transforms unstructured information into a searchable semantic representation. This representation powers vector databases, retrieval systems, agent memory, enterprise knowledge access, MCP-backed resources, and Retrieval-Augmented Generation architectures. Understanding how embeddings, similarity search, and chunking work together is essential for designing scalable and reliable Claude-based AI systems.
